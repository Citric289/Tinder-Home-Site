The city-versus-suburbs decision is one of the most personal calls a Chicago-area buyer makes. It's not really a question of better or worse — it's a question of which trade-offs you're willing to live with for the next decade. After helping hundreds of families on both sides of this choice, I've noticed the conversations tend to come down to four practical levers.

## 1. The Commute Math

If you're commuting downtown three or more days a week, geography matters more than zip-code prestige. The North Line Metra to Park Ridge, Edison Park, or Glenview can land you at Ogilvie in 25 to 35 minutes — often faster than driving from Lincoln Square in rush hour. But for two- or three-day hybrid schedules, the math changes. Sometimes the right move is a smaller city condo and a bigger suburban yard you grow into later.

## 2. Schools You'll Actually Use

Buyers without school-age kids still pay attention to schools — because future resale will. In the city, magnet, selective enrollment, and private options blur the simple ranking story. In the suburbs, your address and your school district are essentially the same conversation. Park Ridge, Glenview District 34, Hinsdale 181, and Wilmette 39 all command a premium because the schools are predictable. That premium gets paid back at resale.

## 3. Walkability and Daily Life

Walking to dinner, coffee, or the grocery store is the thing my suburb-to-city movers miss least often. Many of Chicago's near-north and north suburbs have invested heavily in downtown cores — Park Ridge's Uptown, downtown Evanston, downtown Glenview — that capture much of what people love about city blocks at a smaller scale. Visit on a Saturday morning before you decide it has to be the city.

## 4. Total Cost of Living, Not Just Price

Suburban Cook County property taxes can be eye-opening — sometimes 2.0% to 2.5% of assessed value. A $900K Park Ridge home and a $900K Lincoln Park condo carry very different annual carrying costs. Build the full picture: mortgage, taxes, insurance, parking, HOA, and the lifestyle costs that follow your zip code. The right answer is the one you can sustain comfortably.

> Buy the lifestyle you'll actually live — not the one your friends are posting about.

## A Framework I Use With Clients

Before showings, I ask buyers to rank five things in order: commute, schools, square footage, walkability, and total monthly cost. Whatever lands in your top two should drive your search. Whatever's in your bottom two becomes your flex room. This sounds simple — it is — but it cuts months off the average search.

If you're somewhere in the middle of this decision, I'm happy to walk you through both sides honestly. I sell in the city, the close-in suburbs, and increasingly on Florida's Gulf Coast — I have no incentive to push you in one direction over another. The right answer is whichever one you'll be glad about three years from now.
